Dance Styles Around the World

üíÉ Hip Hop & Street Dance (USA)

Born in the 1970s in New York City. A expression of Black and Latino culture. History: Started in neighborhoods as a way to channel energy positively instead of violence. Kids love this style for its freedom and attitude.

Cultural note: Hip hop is not just dance‚Äîit's a complete culture including music, art, fashion. When teaching it, acknowledge its origins and cultural significance.

ü•Å Afrobeat & African Dance (West Africa)

Rooted in West African traditions. Hip-heavy, rhythmic, grounded. History: Celebration, storytelling, and community bonding. Fela Kuti modernized it in the 1960s. Kids feel the joy and energy in every step.

Cultural note: Many African dances are connected to celebrations, rituals, and social bonding. Teach the joy behind the movement.

üíÉ Latin Dance (Mexico, Puerto Rico, Cuba, Colombia)

Salsa, bachata, reggaeton, cumbia. Hip-focused with partner connection. History: Blend of African, Spanish, and Caribbean influences. Born in communities celebrating love and joy. Kids love the energy and rhythm.

Cultural note: Many Latin dances emphasize partnership and connection. Teach them as expressions of celebration and togetherness.

üéµ K-Pop & Asian Pop (Korea, Japan, Southeast Asia)

Highly choreographed, precise, synchronized. History: Modern Korean and Japanese entertainment industry. Blends traditional and contemporary. Kids love the precision and pop culture element.

Cultural note: K-pop is a global phenomenon. It's modern, it's inclusive, and it represents Asian culture in mainstream media. Support it.

üé≠ Contemporary & Ballet (Global)

Ballet has European roots. Contemporary is modern, experimental, personal. History: Ballet: 15th century Italy, refined in France. Contemporary: 20th century reaction to classical technique. Both are powerful forms.

Cultural note: Ballet has been less inclusive historically. Today, dancers of color are breaking barriers. Celebrate representation.

How to Use DanceWithKids for Cultural Education

Plan: Monthly Cultural Focus

January: Hip Hop & Street Dance

Pick hip hop routines. Research the origins. Talk about Black and Latino culture. Watch age-appropriate documentaries.

February: African & Afrobeat

Learn Afrobeat routines. Discuss West African culture. Listen to traditional and modern music.

March: Latin Dance

Latin dances. Celebrate Latin American countries. Try different styles (salsa, bachata, etc).

Teaching Cultural Respect

‚ùå What NOT to Do

  • Don't use dance as a costume or stereotype
  • Don't claim credit for traditional dances
  • Don't make fun of accents or cultural elements
  • Don't treat cultural dances as "exotic" or "other"
  • Don't ignore the history and meaning

‚úÖ What TO Do

  • Teach the history and cultural context
  • Celebrate the origins and artists
  • Respect the movements and their meanings
  • If your child is from that culture, let them lead
  • Listen to artists from that culture
  • Support creators from that community
  • Acknowledge when styles have been appropriated

Representation Matters

When a child sees someone who looks like them dancing, something magical happens. They imagine themselves as powerful, joyful, talented. That representation changes everything.

At DanceWithKids, our instructors and dancers represent:

  • Different skin tones and ethnicities
  • Different body types and abilities
  • Different ages and family structures
  • Different gender expressions
  • Different cultural backgrounds

Every child should see someone who looks like them on screen, having fun, moving confidently, being celebrated.
