Dance as an Emotional Regulation Tool for Kids

← Back to Blog

A six-year-old is melting down in the grocery store. A nine-year-old slams their bedroom door after a conflict with a sibling. A four-year-old is vibrating with anxiety before their first day of preschool. These scenarios are not signs of "bad" behavior. They are signs that a child's emotional experience has exceeded their current capacity to regulate it. And that capacity, like any other developmental skill, can be grown.

As a child psychologist, I have spent my career studying how children develop the ability to manage their emotional states. One of the most promising and underutilized tools in this domain is dance. Not dance therapy administered by a licensed therapist, though that has its place, but simple, accessible movement practices that parents and educators can use in everyday life to help children build stronger emotional regulation skills.

"Emotions live in the body before they reach the brain. When we help children move their bodies intentionally, we give them a direct pathway to processing what they feel before it overwhelms them."

How Movement Processes Emotions

To understand why dance helps with emotional regulation, we need to understand how emotions work in the body. Emotions are not purely mental events. They are physiological experiences that manifest through changes in heart rate, muscle tension, breathing patterns, and neurochemical balance. When a child feels angry, their fists clench, their jaw tightens, their breathing becomes shallow, and adrenaline floods their system. When they feel anxious, their stomach churns, their muscles tense, and cortisol elevates.

Traditional approaches to emotional regulation often focus on the cognitive layer: "Take a deep breath," "Count to ten," "Use your words." These strategies are valuable, but they require the prefrontal cortex, the brain's rational thinking center, to override the limbic system, the brain's emotional center. In children under twelve, the prefrontal cortex is still developing, which means cognitive regulation strategies often fail precisely when they are needed most, during moments of intense emotion.

Dance takes a different approach. Instead of asking the brain to control the body, dance works through the body to influence the brain. When a child moves their body in specific, intentional ways, they directly alter the physiological state that is fueling their emotional experience. Vigorous movement burns off adrenaline and cortisol. Rhythmic movement activates the parasympathetic nervous system, promoting calm. Expansive movement releases muscle tension. Slow, flowing movement regulates breathing. The body leads, and the brain follows.

Five Dance Techniques for Emotional Regulation

Technique 1: Shake It Off

Ages: 3+ For: Anger, frustration, excess energy Duration: 2-3 minutes

This technique is exactly what it sounds like. When a child is flooded with anger, frustration, or excess energy, invite them to literally shake it off. Start with the hands: shake them vigorously for 10 seconds. Then add the arms, then the shoulders, then the head (gently), then the whole torso, then the legs and feet. Build to a full-body shake where every part of the body is moving. Then gradually slow down, reducing the shaking piece by piece until the child is standing still.

The physiological mechanism is straightforward: vigorous shaking releases muscle tension, burns off adrenaline, and activates the body's natural relaxation response through the contrast between intense activation and subsequent stillness. Many mammals naturally shake after a stressful encounter to discharge the fight-or-flight response. Children can learn to do the same thing deliberately.

When to use it

After a conflict with a sibling, when homework frustration is building, when a child says "I'm so mad!" and cannot articulate further. Best used before the emotional peak, when you see early signs of escalation.

Technique 2: Freeze Dance for Impulse Control

Ages: 4+ For: Impulsivity, hyperactivity, difficulty stopping Duration: 5-10 minutes

Freeze dance is not just a party game. It is a powerful impulse control training tool. The fundamental skill it teaches is inhibitory control: the ability to stop a behavior that is already in motion. This is the same skill that allows a child to resist grabbing a toy from another child, to stop talking when the teacher raises their hand, or to pause before reacting to a provocation.

Play music and encourage wild, energetic dancing. When the music stops, the child must freeze instantly. Start with long dance periods and short freezes. Gradually shorten the dance periods and lengthen the freezes. Over time, the child strengthens the neural pathways that connect "stop signal" to "stop action," making impulse control easier in all contexts, not just on the dance floor.

When to use it

As a regular practice (three times per week) rather than in the moment of crisis. Impulse control is built through repetition over time. Treat it as exercise for the brain's braking system. Also effective as a transition activity between high-energy and calm activities.

Technique 3: Mirror Dancing for Empathy

Ages: 5+ For: Social difficulties, empathy building, attunement Duration: 5-8 minutes

Two people face each other. One leads, one follows, mirroring every movement as precisely as possible. The leader must move slowly enough for the follower to keep up. The follower must watch intently and respond in real time. After two minutes, switch roles. Then try a final round with no designated leader, where both partners try to move together spontaneously.

Mirror dancing develops empathy through three mechanisms. First, it requires sustained attention to another person's body and movements, building the observational skills that underlie empathic awareness. Second, the mirror neuron system activates when copying another person's movements, creating a visceral sense of shared experience. Third, the leaderless round requires each person to both express and respond simultaneously, developing the reciprocal attunement that is the foundation of healthy relationships.

When to use it

Between siblings who are struggling to get along. Between a parent and child who need to rebuild connection after conflict. In classroom settings to build social cohesion. Particularly powerful for children who struggle to read social cues or who have difficulty with perspective-taking.

Technique 4: Flowing River for Anxiety

Ages: 4+ For: Anxiety, nervousness, pre-event stress Duration: 3-5 minutes

Put on slow, flowing music (instrumental works best). Invite the child to imagine they are water in a river. Start standing still like a calm lake. Then begin to sway gently, like a breeze crossing the water's surface. Gradually build to larger, flowing movements: arms sweeping like currents, body rocking like gentle waves. Move through the room slowly, "flowing" around obstacles. Then gradually return to stillness.

This technique works because anxiety is characterized by muscle tension, shallow breathing, and a sense of rigidity. Flowing movement directly counteracts each of these: the continuous motion prevents muscle clenching, the rhythmic quality naturally deepens breathing, and the fluid imagery gives the child a mental model of flexibility and ease. The water metaphor also helps children externalize their experience: "I am not anxious; the water in me is choppy, and I can calm it."

When to use it

Before anxiety-provoking events like doctor visits, first days, or performances. During moments of general unease when a child says "my tummy feels funny." At bedtime when worries tend to intensify. Can also be used proactively as a daily calming practice.

Technique 5: Stomp and Ground

Ages: 3+ For: Dissociation, emotional numbness, feeling "floaty" Duration: 2-4 minutes

Some children respond to overwhelming emotions by shutting down rather than acting out. They go quiet, still, and "somewhere else." For these children, grounding through the feet is especially effective. Stand barefoot if possible. Begin stomping slowly and firmly, feeling each foot make contact with the floor. Increase the intensity gradually. Add a rhythm: stomp-stomp-clap, stomp-stomp-clap. Then add voice: count each stomp out loud, or add sounds like "ha!" or "hey!" with each impact.

The percussive contact with the ground sends strong proprioceptive signals up through the body, literally pulling the child's nervous system back into present-moment awareness. The addition of voice activates the vagus nerve, which regulates the parasympathetic nervous system. Together, these inputs help a dissociated child re-enter their body and their present experience in a safe, controlled way.

When to use it

When a child appears "checked out" or emotionally flat after a difficult experience. When transitioning from screen time to real-world engagement. When a child seems unable to identify what they are feeling. Particularly valuable for children who tend to internalize rather than externalize emotions.

When Dance Helps vs. When Professional Help Is Needed

Dance-based emotional regulation is a powerful preventive and supportive tool, but it is not a substitute for professional mental health support. The techniques in this article are designed for typical childhood emotional challenges. Consider consulting a child psychologist or therapist if you observe any of the following.

  • Emotional outbursts are increasing in frequency, intensity, or duration over time rather than improving
  • A child's emotional responses seem significantly out of proportion to the situation consistently
  • Emotional difficulties are interfering with the child's ability to attend school, maintain friendships, or participate in daily activities
  • The child expresses persistent feelings of hopelessness, worthlessness, or a desire to hurt themselves
  • Physical symptoms like chronic stomachaches, headaches, or sleep disruption accompany emotional difficulties
  • Emotional dysregulation follows a specific traumatic event or significant life change
  • You feel that the intensity of your child's emotions is beyond what you can support alone

In these situations, dance and movement can still play a valuable complementary role alongside professional treatment. Many therapists incorporate movement into their practice. But the professional assessment, diagnosis, and treatment planning that a qualified therapist provides cannot be replaced by movement practices alone. Seeking help is not a sign of failure. It is a sign that you are paying attention and taking your child's wellbeing seriously.

Building an Emotional Regulation Practice

The key to using dance for emotional regulation is consistency. Like any skill, emotional regulation improves with regular practice. Rather than waiting until a child is in crisis and then introducing a technique, build these movements into daily routines when emotions are neutral. A two-minute morning shake-off, a weekly freeze dance session, or a bedtime flowing river practice teaches children the movements when they are calm so the movements are available when they are not.

Over time, many children begin to self-select these techniques without prompting. I have seen children walk away from a frustrating puzzle, shake their hands vigorously for 30 seconds, and return to the task with renewed focus. I have watched siblings resolve a conflict by spontaneously engaging in mirror dancing. When movement-based regulation becomes part of a child's toolkit, they carry it with them for life.

The body knows how to heal. Dance simply gives it permission.
